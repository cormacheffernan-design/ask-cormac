## 6. Investment structure **[C]**

**Allocation: 40% Liquidity Fund / 50% Credit Guarantee Fund / 10% Bank instruments.** One pool of largely-liquid capital earns from three streams — "efficiency, not gearing": deposit rate (on the bank sleeve), facility/commitment fees (Liquidity Fund), and credit-guarantee fees (Credit Guarantee Fund).

The two credit-bearing sleeves run through **two separate underlying funds** — do not merge them.

### 6a. Liquidity Fund (`FALF01`)
40% sleeve. Earns underwriting fees and yield **via Finture Alternative Investments**: Finture underwrites senior-secured/asset-backed credit at 98c per $1.00 par, sells the majority down to wholesale at par (approx. 2% spread), and retains 15–25% (target 20%) as inventory earning a 7.50% par coupon. Engine target **RBA + 4% net (approx. 8.4%)**; 1.00% all-in, no performance fee; $1.00 unit price held flat; monthly dealing (2nd Tuesday). **RBA + 4% is the engine, not the investor payout.**

### 6b. Credit Guarantee Fund + the Phycom/Finplex diesel facility
50% sleeve — the largest. It is a **separate dedicated fund** (separate from the Liquidity Fund). Structure is **fund-of-fund**:

> **Fund EQ → the (separate) Credit Guarantee Fund → the institutional Phycom/Finplex diesel trade-finance structure.**

Fund EQ is **one layer removed** — it holds units in the intermediate fund, not the underlying diesel deal. This keeps retail Fund EQ money out of direct contact with the wholesale-only Phycom facility.

**How the underlying deal works (and why it's low-risk):** it is a contingent **standby guarantee**, not deployed trade finance. Capital sits as **blocked funds backing a bank instrument**, not as advanced cash. It is drawn only when a guarantee is actually triggered — utilisation is **sub-5% (around 10% as a conservative case) on a rolling 12-month basis** (e.g. a facility "in use" for around 10 days a year; "on standby" the rest). Even when drawn, loss-given-draw is structurally low: 100% cash-collateral before instrument issuance, Delivery-versus-Payment, an **AA-rated off-taker as buyer of last resort**, 2% supplier performance bonds, SGS/Intertek inspection, and step-in rights. Counterparty is **AGE (Con Varvaris)**; operator is Phycom/Finplex (Fuel Flow Exchange).

## 7. Liquidity & redemptions **[C]**

**The honest cash hierarchy:** 10% genuine cash sleeve / approx. 22% hard cash / 67% look-through (the 45-point gap is contingent credit-guarantee capacity counted as liquid, which is fair because it's blocked/standby approx. 95% of the time).

**EQ Access Now is "processed daily, settlement may be delayed."** Units are redeemed daily (off the register); cash settlement is prompt in normal two-way flow but can lag in extreme conditions. Internal analogy: like a bank — your money, but a run can delay settlement. (Internal only.)

**Redemption defence, in order:**
1. 10% genuine cash sleeve.
2. The Liquidity Fund and Credit Guarantee Fund manage liquidity **up** into the cash allocation on demand (a top-up mechanism — there is **no inter-investor priority** and **no preferential redemption right** inside the underlying managers).
3. **EQ Plus 90's 90-day lock-up** is the stable base. New Plus 90 applications are directed to the less-liquid holdings, which **frees liquid capacity** to meet daily redemptions — so Plus 90 indirectly supports Access Now.
4. Backstop: "processed daily, settlement may be delayed" + the constitution's payment-flexibility provisions.

Illustrative scenario (one of many; manager acts with discretion): $5M in the daily fund, $4M of redemptions in a day — check immediate underlying liquidity, move it to the cash sleeve to settle. **Stress case** the model must own: a daily-redemption spike at the same time as stalled Plus 90 inflows.

## 8. Economics & fees **[C]**

**Fund EQ's revenue = the 1.62% retained MER + its performance share (subordinated units).** It does NOT capture the Liquidity Fund's underwriting spread/coupon — those accrue to Finture at the underlying layer.

**The waterfall:** underlying funds (incl. Liquidity Fund) earn yield → pay their own fees → deliver a **net yield up to Fund EQ** → which must clear, in order, **(1) investor target return, (2) Fund EQ MER (1.62%), (3) Fund EQ performance fee.** "The fund earns 8.4%" is wrong as an investor statement — that yield is consumed by the waterfall.

**Performance fee = subordinated-units buffer [P, mechanics to re-confirm].** Outperformance is issued to the manager as subordinated units ranking behind ordinary units; they top up investor distributions during underperformance (high-water mark applies). It is the **shock absorber that defends the target return when the waterfall is thin**, not just upside capture. Units, not debt (debt risks Div 974 recharacterisation and RG 45 Benchmark 2 breach).
