## 6. Investment structure

Allocation: **40% Liquidity Fund / 50% Credit Guarantee Fund / 10% Bank instruments.** One pool of largely-liquid capital earns from three streams — efficiency, not gearing: deposit rate on the bank sleeve, facility and commitment fees on the Liquidity Fund, and credit-guarantee fees on the Credit Guarantee Fund.

The two credit-bearing sleeves run through two separate underlying funds — don't merge them.

### 6a. Liquidity Fund (FALF01)
40% sleeve. Earns underwriting fees and yield via Finture Alternative Investments: Finture underwrites senior-secured and asset-backed credit at 98c per $1.00 par, sells the majority down to wholesale at par (approx. 2% spread) and retains 15 to 25% (target 20%) as inventory earning a 7.50% par coupon. ### 6b. Credit Guarantee Fund + the Phycom/Finplex diesel facility
50% sleeve, the largest. It is a separate dedicated fund, fund-of-fund in structure:

> Cash Plus Income → the (separate) Credit Guarantee Fund → the institutional Phycom/Finplex diesel trade-finance structure.

The fund is one layer removed — it holds units in the intermediate fund, not the underlying diesel deal. This keeps retail money out of direct contact with the wholesale-only Phycom facility.

Why it's low-risk: it is a contingent standby guarantee, not deployed trade finance. Capital sits as blocked funds backing a bank instrument, not as advanced cash, and is drawn only when a guarantee is actually triggered — utilisation is sub-5% (around 10% as a conservative case) on a rolling 12-month basis. Even when drawn, loss-given-draw is structurally low: 100% cash-collateral before instrument issuance, Delivery-versus-Payment, an AA-rated off-taker as buyer of last resort, 2% supplier performance bonds, SGS/Intertek inspection, and step-in rights. Counterparty is AGE (Con Varvaris); operator is Phycom/Finplex (Fuel Flow Exchange).

## 7. Liquidity & redemptions

The honest cash hierarchy: 10% genuine cash sleeve; approx. 22% hard cash; 67% look-through (the 45-point gap is contingent credit-guarantee capacity counted as liquid, which is fair because it's blocked or on standby approx. 95% of the time).

Cash Plus (at call) is "processed daily, settlement may be delayed." "At call" is the tier name, not a settlement promise. Units are redeemed daily off the register; cash settlement is prompt in normal two-way flow but can lag in extreme conditions.

Approved answer to "is it safe / is it a deposit?": "It's not a deposit and not government-guaranteed. It's an investment built to behave like a high-liquidity cash alternative. Capital is at risk, but the structure defends it in layers: the 10% genuine cash sleeve, the top-up mechanism from the underlying funds, the notice-period base under Income Plus (30-day) and High Income Plus (90-day), and the subordinated-units buffer that absorbs the first shortfall on returns. Redemptions are processed daily; settlement can lag in extreme conditions." Keep clear of the banned redemption phrases and never present the fund as a deposit.

Redemption defence, in order:
1. The 10% genuine cash sleeve.
2. The Liquidity Fund and Credit Guarantee Fund manage liquidity up into the cash allocation on demand — a top-up mechanism, with no inter-investor priority and no preferential redemption right inside the underlying managers.
3. The notice tiers are the stable base. New Income Plus (30-day) and High Income Plus (90-day) applications are directed to the less-liquid holdings, which frees liquid capacity to meet daily redemptions. High Income Plus is the deepest base; Income Plus sits between it and at-call.
4. Backstop: "processed daily, settlement may be delayed," plus the constitution's payment-flexibility provisions.

Illustrative: $5M in the daily tier, $4M of redemptions in a day — check immediate underlying liquidity and move it to the cash sleeve to settle. Stress case to own: a daily-redemption spike at the same time as stalled notice-tier inflows.

## 8. Economics & fees

The fund's revenue is the 1.62% retained MER plus its performance share (subordinated units). It does not capture the Liquidity Fund's underwriting spread or coupon — those accrue to Finture at the underlying layer.

The waterfall: underlying funds earn yield, pay their own fees, and deliver a net yield up to the fund, which must clear, in order: (1) investor target return, (2) MER (1.62%), (3) performance fee. "The fund earns 8.4%" is wrong as an investor statement — that yield is consumed by the waterfall.

The performance fee is a subordinated-units buffer. Outperformance is issued to the manager as subordinated units ranking behind ordinary units; they top up investor distributions during underperformance (high-water mark applies). It is the shock absorber that defends the target return when the waterfall is thin, not just upside capture. Units, not debt.

## 14. Sales strategy

**Lead with this.** Right now we raise by doing deals. The live priority is the anchor-first bootstrap: win a small number of large, single-controller cash pools to punch through to $50M-plus before the retail channels carry the load. That is the current phase, not the permanent shape — it sits inside the standing four-quadrant channel model (Section 15) as the Toddler phase (below). Staff asking "what's the sales strategy" today should hear "do deals, win anchors," framed as the phase we're in, not as how the fund raises money forever.

**The anchor-first bootstrap.** The filter is control and speed, not entity type: anyone who can move a large cash balance quickly on their own authority — family offices, fund managers, corporate treasuries, SMSF aggregators, adviser IMAs, foundation or MIS cash. Anchors are won on rebates and personal relationships. All deal structures are on the table. Every deal routes to the CEO for approval. Whatever is struck carries a hard sunset of 31 March 2027: the deal ends and is renegotiated then — a renewal gate, not a reversion to standard terms. Pipeline is currently a few institutions and advisers in negotiation, unnamed for now. Origination is everyone's job — all associated with FundEQ carry contacts and are expected to bring anchors in.

**The four growth phases.** Same fund, different game at each size:
1. Toddler — up to $100M, Jun to Dec 2026. Deal-driven, chunky, relationship-led inflows that won't look like steady-state flows and don't need to. Get organised, get oriented. This is the anchor-first phase we're in.
2. Childhood — up to $300M, 2027. Earn the ratings, sign the agreements: platforms, dealer groups, white-label partners.
3. Strong Adult — up to $1bn, 2027 to 2028. Brand, loyalty, moat — make FundEQ the default name for enhanced cash and make leaving costly.
4. Fat Middle-aged Man — up to $5bn, 2029 and beyond. Maximum FUM, minimum effort; flows arrive by reputation and operating leverage does the work.

Don't mistake toddler chaos for the firm's character. Judge the trajectory, not the table manners.

**The five buyer pillars.** Each is an access point to the underlying investor, or to whoever controls the cash decision in the case of fund managers and discretionary advisers:
1. Direct market — pre-mortgage savers who want the highest rate but need deposit-ready access and don't want to babysit bonus-rate conditions. The lead direct persona. Owned through O3 (Jesse and Chee).
2. Advisers — adding to portfolio yield rather than carrying cash as a drag on performance.
3. Fund managers — same logic as advisers.
4. SMSF and other administrators — where they hold sway over the cash, sold on the functionality.
5. White-labels — into sectors the banks under-serve.

Tier choice (Cash Plus / Income Plus / High Income Plus) follows the investor's own needs, not the channel, and one investor will often sit across multiple options. There are no minimums — any ticket, retail or anchor.

**What we win against, and the real prize.** We sit where portfolio and household cash actually sits — against bank savings and cash management trusts (liquid, low yield, approx. 0 to 1.5%), high-yield savings accounts (honeymoon rates and setup-to-fail conditions, approx. 4%) and term deposits (locked in, roll risk at maturity, approx. 4 to 5%). Deliberately not against credit funds — those are framed as opaque, return not paying for the risk. The strategic prize is the deposit pool, not the managed-fund market: approx. $1.72tn in Australian household deposits (APRA, Jan 2026), against all listed cash ETFs combined at approx. $6.5bn, which is 0.4% of the pool. The capture maths: $100M is 0.006% of household deposits (our Dec 2026 target); $200M is 0.012% (Year 1 inflow expectation); $1bn is 0.06%. We don't need to beat the ETF incumbents — we need a sliver of the bank account. (Market-sizing figures are the June 2026 workshop basis, indicative and pending confirmation.)

The whitespace: no competitor combines enhanced yield, at-call access and a spend mechanism. Why deposit money is winnable — the big four hold around 70% of banking assets and are rate-apathetic (CBA alone holds around 27% of household deposits); the yield gap over bank-panel rates is structural (AAA returned 3.92% over the year to March 2026); switching friction is the moat we remove, because at-call redemption plus a card that spends directly from the fund removes the reason not to switch; and trust is buildable, not inherited. The differentiator is transparency — "Trust through Transparency."

**Objection handling — "how do I know you're not the next First Guardian or Shield?"** Those collapses died of three things: opacity, illiquidity and related-party self-dealing with no independent check. We invert all three, and that is the answer:
1. Independent, licensed RE — Cache RE Services (AFSL 514360). The issuer and RE is a third party; the manager can't move money on its own say-so.
2. Independent custody — Perpetual Corporate Trust holds the assets; a bankruptcy-remote registered scheme.
3. Independent IC with delegated authority — the Board can't override individual investment decisions; the Board's only power is dissolution.
4. Full look-through — the AI-enabled data room lets an investor interrogate holdings down the capital stack. The direct antidote to the opacity that sank the others.
5. Manager's own capital first — the subordinated-units buffer means the manager absorbs underperformance before any investor.
6. The liquidity anchor — the 10% genuine cash sleeve as first line of defence, backed by the layered liquidity architecture in Section 7. (Never present the 67% look-through figure as cash on hand.)
7. Independent validation — Foresight STRONG, plus the audited nine-year redemptions-and-distributions history (Grant Thornton), always caveated as performance under prior management.

The one-liner: "Trust through Transparency" is built as the answer to that exact question — independent RE and custody, full look-through, and our own money first in the firing line.
