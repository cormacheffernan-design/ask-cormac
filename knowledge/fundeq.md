## 7. Liquidity & redemptions

The honest cash hierarchy: 10% genuine cash sleeve; approx. 22% hard cash; 67% look-through (the 45-point gap is contingent credit-guarantee capacity counted as liquid, which is fair because it's blocked or on standby approx. 95% of the time).

Cash Plus is the genuine cash option — 100% liquid bank deposit instruments in the Cash Fund — so for Cash Plus we can call it cash and promise daily liquidity at call. "At call" is used only for the Cash Plus (cash) option. The conservative "redemptions processed daily; settlement may be delayed in extreme conditions" framing applies to the two income options and to any fund-wide statement over the credit-bearing sleeves; the extreme-conditions settlement provision remains available at the constitution level as a backstop.

Approved answer to "is it safe / is it a deposit?": "It's not a deposit and not government-guaranteed. It's an investment built to behave like a high-liquidity cash alternative. Capital is at risk, but the structure defends it in layers: the 10% genuine cash sleeve, the top-up mechanism from the underlying funds, the notice-period base under Income Plus (30-day) and High Income Plus (90-day), and the subordinated-units buffer that absorbs the first shortfall on returns. Redemptions are processed daily; settlement can lag in extreme conditions." Keep clear of the banned redemption phrases and never present the fund as a deposit.

Redemption defence, in order:
1. The 10% genuine cash sleeve.
2. The Liquidity Fund and Credit Guarantee Fund manage liquidity up into the cash allocation on demand — a top-up mechanism, with no inter-investor priority and no preferential redemption right inside the underlying managers.
3. The notice tiers are the stable base. New Income Plus (30-day) and High Income Plus (90-day) applications are directed to the less-liquid holdings, which frees liquid capacity to meet daily redemptions. High Income Plus is the deepest base; Income Plus sits between it and at-call.
4. Backstop: "processed daily, settlement may be delayed," plus the constitution's payment-flexibility provisions.

Illustrative: $5M in the daily tier, $4M of redemptions in a day — check immediate underlying liquidity and move it to the cash sleeve to settle. Stress case to own: a daily-redemption spike at the same time as stalled notice-tier inflows.

## 8. Economics & fees

The fund's revenue is the 1.62% retained MER plus its performance share (subordinated units). It does not capture the Liquidity Fund's underwriting spread or coupon — those accrue to Finture at the underlying layer.

The 1.62% MER and the performance fee apply to the two income options only. No MER is taken on Cash Plus: Cash Plus investors receive the flat 0.5% straight from the Cash Fund deposit rate, and the MER and waterfall below describe the income options, not the cash option. Cash Plus earns the manager nothing — it is a deliberate loss-leader to win the relationship and the balance, with the economics coming later as investors move into the income options (and through the other channels). Internally, treat Cash Plus as customer acquisition, not a revenue line.

The waterfall: underlying funds earn yield, pay their own fees, and deliver a net yield up to the fund, which must clear, in order: (1) investor target return, (2) MER (1.62%), (3) performance fee. "The fund earns 8.4%" is wrong as an investor statement — that yield is consumed by the waterfall.

The performance fee is a subordinated-units buffer. Outperformance is issued to the manager as subordinated units ranking behind ordinary units; they top up investor distributions during underperformance (high-water mark applies). It is the shock absorber that defends the target return when the waterfall is thin, not just upside capture. Units, not debt.
